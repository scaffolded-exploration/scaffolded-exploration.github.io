Diversity Over Frequency: Rethinking Tool Use in Visual Chain-of-Thought Agents

Visual tools help most as training-time scaffolding through broader exploration, not through persistent tool usage.

Dong-Hee Kim1, Reuben Tan2, Donghyun Kim1
1Korea University · 2Microsoft Research
Paper Code Coming soon arXiv Coming soon BibTeX
Overview. Early diverse exploration acts as scaffolding: simply increasing tool frequency is not enough, but encouraging broader exploration improves final reasoning even when tool use later fades.
Paper summary

Abstract

This project page summarizes a study of tool-use collapse in visual chain-of-thought agents and argues that the main benefit of tools comes from exploration during training rather than frequency of use at inference time.

Visual agents employ external visual tools within visual chains of thought to incorporate fine-grained evidence. While prior work has mainly studied these tools in visual search settings, this paper investigates harder tasks including 3D spatial reasoning and medical visual question answering, where local tool-acquired evidence must be integrated with the global visual context.

The central finding is a tool-use collapse phenomenon: under vanilla reinforcement fine-tuning, accuracy improves while tool use falls toward zero. Completely banning tools hurts performance, but explicitly incentivizing tool use yields only marginal gains despite much higher tool frequency.

The paper further shows that both vanilla training and reward-based tool encouragement reduce rollout diversity, whereas adaptive entropy regularization preserves broader exploration and achieves the best performance. This supports a training-time view of tools as scaffolding: what matters is the richer exploration history they enable, not simply persistent tool invocation.

Core result

Main results

Figure 2 is the key quantitative result of the paper: tool-use frequency and reasoning performance move differently, and exploration-aware training changes that relationship.

Figure 2 · Training dynamics of tool use and accuracy

Vanilla RFT and reward-based tool encouragement show sharply different tool-use behavior but similarly limited gains. Entropy-regularized training improves performance more substantially, even though tool use still declines late in training.

Mechanism

Exploration analysis

The performance gap comes from diversity in reasoning and crop selection, rather than raw tool frequency alone.

Figure 3 · Textual exploration stays diverse only with entropy regularization

Distinct n-gram ratios decrease steadily for vanilla RFT and reward-based tool encouragement, indicating more repetitive reasoning. Entropy regularization maintains higher textual diversity throughout training.

Figure 4 · Visual exploration becomes broader and more relevant

At an early training checkpoint, the entropy-regularized model explores regions tied to the query context, while the vanilla policy fixates more narrowly on the most salient subject.

Why this matters

  • Text space: more diverse pre-grounding reasoning reduces premature convergence to templated thought patterns.
  • Visual space: broader crop exploration improves coverage without sacrificing semantic relevance.
  • Interpretation: tools help most when they broaden the training trajectory, not when they are forced at inference time.
Transfer

Generalization

The same qualitative asymmetry extends beyond the main 3D spatial reasoning setting and appears again in a broader tool suite on medical VQA.

Figure 5 · Similar dynamics on OpenThinkIMG and VQA-RAD

In a heterogeneous visual-tool setting, vanilla and entropy-regularized training both reduce tool use over time, while reward-based encouragement makes the policy rely on tools almost always.

Reading the transfer result

The best-performing method is still the one that preserves exploration diversity. That pattern holds more cleanly than a “use more tools” interpretation.
